Lead Scoring & Buying Group Identification

A propensity model and buying group framework for B2B SaaS marketing

View on GitHub

What This Project Does

Imagine you’re running marketing at a B2B software company. You have 100,000 accounts in your CRM — companies that might buy your product. But your team can only actively engage with maybe a few thousand of them this quarter. How do you decide which ones?

This project builds two tools to answer that question:

  1. A propensity model that scores every account from 0 to 100% based on how likely they are to close a deal
  2. A buying group analyzer that looks at accounts that should be closing but aren’t, figures out what’s missing (the right people aren’t involved), and recommends exactly who marketing should go find

Together, they turn a chaotic spreadsheet of accounts into a prioritized action plan.

Why This Matters in the Real World

At Atlassian, I did similar work — analyzing which contact profiles showed up on deals that closed, identifying accounts with coverage gaps, and building enrichment strategies that informed marketing’s contact acquisition priorities.

This project recreates that methodology on synthetic data so I can walk through the full approach openly, from data generation to model evaluation to strategic recommendations.

A note on synthetic data: The data generator embeds known signals at controlled strengths — for example, VP+ contacts on a deal increase win probability. The model’s job is to recover those signals from the feature matrix, validating that the pipeline works end-to-end. This demonstrates methodology, not empirical discovery. The same pipeline on real CRM data would face additional noise, confounders, and temporal dynamics — but the engineering and evaluation framework would carry over directly.

The Data: What a CRM Looks Like

Before we get into the model, it helps to understand what data B2B marketing teams actually work with. The project generates synthetic data across four tables that mirror what you’d find in a real CRM (Customer Relationship Management) system like Salesforce:

Accounts (100,000 rows)

Each row is a company. We know their industry (Technology, Healthcare, etc.), size (employee count, annual revenue), segment (SMB, Mid-Market, or Enterprise), and tech stack — which software tools they already use. We also know if they’re already a customer of ours.

Contacts (717,820 rows)

Each row is a person at one of those companies. We know their job title, job function (Engineering, Marketing, Sales, etc.), seniority (from Individual Contributor up to C-Suite), and how they entered our system (paid search, organic, events, etc.).

Opportunities (50,000 rows)

Each row is a potential deal. It’s tied to an account and has a stage (Discovery → Evaluation → Proposal → Negotiation → Closed Won or Closed Lost), a dollar amount, and an outcome — did we win or lose?

Contact-Opportunity Bridge (122,677 rows)

This is the crucial table. It tells us which people are involved in which deals, and what role they play: Champion (our internal advocate), Decision Maker (signs the check), Evaluator (tests the product), Influencer (shapes opinion), or Blocker (raises objections).

Feature Engineering: Turning Raw Data Into Signals

Raw CRM data isn’t directly useful for a model. A company name or a job title string doesn’t mean anything mathematically. Feature engineering is the process of transforming raw data into numeric signals that a model can learn from.

We build features at three levels, from weakest to strongest:

Level 1: Firmographic & Technographic Features

“What kind of company is this?”

  • Segment encoding — We convert “SMB / Mid-Market / Enterprise” into three separate yes/no columns. Why not just use 1/2/3? Because the relationship isn’t linear — Mid-Market actually has the highest win rate, not Enterprise. Separate columns let the model learn each segment’s unique effect.
  • Log-transformed size — Employee count and revenue are heavily skewed (most companies are small, a few are enormous). A log transform compresses this range so the model doesn’t treat a 20,000-person company as 400x more important than a 50-person one. The insight is that going from 50 to 500 employees matters more than going from 5,000 to 5,500.
  • Tech stack signals — We parse the comma-separated list of tools and create two features: a count of “high-signal” tools (Jira, Salesforce, Slack — tools that suggest the company is in our ecosystem), plus individual flags for the top 10 tools.

Level 2: Contact Composition Features

“Who do we know at this company?”

This is where it gets interesting. We aggregate everyone we know at an account:

  • VP+ count — How many VP or C-Suite contacts do we have? Seniority correlates strongly with deal closure.
  • Function diversity — Are we talking to one department or five? Deals that involve multiple departments are harder to start but more likely to close.
  • Senior density — What fraction of our contacts are senior? An account where 3 of 10 contacts are VPs is different from one where 1 of 50 is.
  • Technical + Business mix — Do we have contacts from both the “builder” side (Engineering, IT, Product) AND the “buyer” side (Marketing, Sales, Finance)? Deals close when both sides are at the table.

Level 3: Buying Group Features

“Who’s actively involved in the sales process?”

The strongest signals come from the deal contact data:

  • Deal contact count — How many distinct people are engaged on active deals?
  • Role coverage — Of the four key roles (Champion, Decision Maker, Evaluator, Influencer), how many are present?
  • Buying group completeness — A composite 0-100 score combining role coverage, seniority presence, function diversity, and technical/business mix

In total, we generate 39 features from these three levels.

A Design Decision: Avoiding Target Leakage

There’s a subtle but important problem here. The Level 3 features — deal contact count, role coverage, buying group completeness — are derived from the same opportunity records whose outcome is the target variable. If we include them in the propensity model, we’re letting the model peek at the answer.

The solution: the propensity model uses only Level 1 and Level 2 features (firmographic, technographic, and contact composition signals that exist before any deal starts). Level 3 features are reserved for the buying group analysis, where we’re describing deal patterns rather than predicting outcomes. This separation is enforced in the code with a include_deal_features flag.

This matters because many portfolio projects (and some production systems) accidentally leak future information into the training set. The result is a model that looks great in evaluation but fails in production, because it relied on information that wouldn’t be available at prediction time.

The Model: Logistic Regression

What Logistic Regression Does

At its core, logistic regression answers a yes/no question: will this account close a deal? It takes all 39 features as inputs and outputs a probability between 0% and 100%.

Think of it like a weighted scorecard. Each feature gets a weight (called a coefficient). Positive weights increase the score; negative weights decrease it. The model learns these weights from historical data — it looks at hundreds of accounts that won or lost, and figures out which features are most predictive.

For example, if the model learns that “VP+ contacts on the deal” has a coefficient of +0.10, it means that having senior contacts engaged makes the account meaningfully more likely to close.

Why Logistic Regression Over Fancier Models

We also trained a Random Forest — a more complex model that builds hundreds of decision trees and averages their predictions. At 100K-account scale, it actually performed slightly worse than logistic regression (AUC 0.567 vs 0.575). We chose logistic regression because:

  • Interpretability: You can look at the coefficients and say “senior density is our strongest signal, existing customer status is second.” Try explaining a 100-tree ensemble to a marketing VP.
  • It’s what production systems use: Marketing platforms like Marketo and HubSpot use logistic regression under the hood for lead scoring. Building with the same approach makes the work directly transferable.
  • No performance justification for complexity: The simpler model matched or beat the fancier one, suggesting the signal structure is additive rather than interaction-driven.

What is Regularization?

Even with ~36K accounts and 39 features, there’s a risk of overfitting — the model memorizes quirks of the training data instead of learning real patterns. Regularization is a technique that penalizes large coefficients, essentially telling the model: “don’t put too much weight on any single feature.”

We use L2 regularization (also called Ridge), controlled by a parameter called C. Lower C = stronger regularization = simpler model. We tested C values of 0.01, 0.1, 1.0, and 10.0, and the model performed best at C=0.01 — fairly strong regularization that keeps feature redundancy (e.g., correlated size and revenue features) from inflating coefficients.

How We Evaluate: Business Metrics That Matter

Technical metrics like accuracy aren’t very useful here. If 64% of accounts lose, a model that always predicts “lose” would be 64% accurate but completely useless. Instead, we evaluate with metrics that answer real business questions.

AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve)

What it is: A number between 0 and 1 that measures how well the model separates winners from losers. An AUC of 0.5 means the model is no better than flipping a coin. An AUC of 1.0 means perfect separation.

Our result: 0.575 (95% CI: 0.56–0.59) on the held-out test set (~7,200 accounts).

What that means: The model is meaningfully better than random. In practice, AUC values of 0.55-0.65 are common for account-level propensity models using only pre-deal features — the signal exists but it’s noisy, because many factors that determine whether a deal closes (budget timing, competitive dynamics, internal politics) aren’t in the data. The tight confidence interval (thanks to the large test set) gives us confidence this isn’t a fluke.

Precision @ K

What it is: If you only look at the top K% of model-scored accounts, what fraction actually won?

Our results:

Top K% Precision What it means
Top 10% 44.8% Nearly half of the highest-scored accounts actually won (vs. 36% baseline)
Top 20% 44.2% Precision holds well beyond the top decile
Top 30% 42.2% Concentrated wins in the top tiers

Why it matters: This is the metric marketing cares about most. “If I can only engage 3,000 accounts this quarter out of 100,000, which ones?” The model says: pick the top-scored ones, and you’ll hit winners 45% of the time instead of 36%.

Lift Chart

What it is: We divide all accounts into 10 equal groups (deciles) based on their score, from highest to lowest. For each group, we measure how much more likely they are to win compared to the overall average. This ratio is called lift.

Our result: The top decile converts at 1.3x the baseline rate. Accounts in the bottom deciles convert well below baseline.

Why it matters: Lift tells you the concentration of value. If the top 30% of scored accounts capture a disproportionate share of wins, you can focus your team’s time there and ignore the rest.

Revenue Capture Curve

What it is: If you work through accounts from highest-scored to lowest, what percentage of total won revenue have you captured at each point?

Why it matters: It answers “how much money am I leaving on the table?” If the top 30% of scored accounts capture 50% of won revenue, the model is doing real work. If it’s close to 30% (what random selection would give you), the model isn’t helping.

Calibration

What it is: Are the predicted probabilities trustworthy? If the model says an account has a 40% chance of closing, does it actually close about 40% of the time?

Why it matters: If you tell a sales team “this account has a 50% chance of closing,” that number needs to be real. A poorly calibrated model might assign 50% to accounts that actually close 20% of the time, which erodes trust.

Feature Importance: What Drives the Score

The beauty of logistic regression is that you can look directly at the coefficients to see what matters. Since this is synthetic data with known signal structure, the model’s job is to recover the embedded signals — and the ranking confirms the pipeline does so correctly:

Top positive drivers (increase propensity):

  • Senior contact density — The fraction of VP+ contacts at an account is the single strongest signal. In real-world B2B sales, senior involvement is widely recognized as the strongest deal predictor.
  • Existing customer status — Upsell/cross-sell opportunities convert at meaningfully higher rates than net-new deals.
  • Complementary tech stack — Accounts that already use tools in our ecosystem (Jira, Salesforce, AWS) score higher.
  • Mid-Market segment — Mid-Market accounts convert at the highest rate, consistent with the “sweet spot” pattern common in B2B SaaS.

Top negative drivers (decrease propensity):

  • SMB segment and certain industries (Manufacturing, Education) — Smaller companies and industries with lower tech adoption close at lower rates.

Both the logistic regression and random forest models agreed on the top signals (senior density, tech stack, max seniority), which validates that the patterns are structural, not artifacts of one particular model type.

Buying Group Analysis

What Is a Buying Group?

In B2B sales, purchasing decisions are rarely made by one person. A typical enterprise deal involves 4-7 people playing different roles:

  • Champion — Your internal advocate who pushes for the deal
  • Decision Maker — The person who signs the check (usually a VP or C-Suite exec)
  • Evaluator — The person who tests the product and assesses technical fit
  • Influencer — Someone whose opinion shapes the decision (could be a respected engineer, a trusted advisor)
  • Blocker — Someone who raises objections or slows the process

A deal is much more likely to close when all four positive roles are filled. If you’re missing a Champion, nobody’s pushing internally. If you’re missing a Decision Maker, there’s no one to sign off.

Completeness Scoring

We score each account’s buying group on a 0-100 scale across four dimensions (25 points each):

Dimension What it measures Full marks if…
Role coverage Key buying roles present All 4 roles filled
Seniority mix Exec + mid-level engagement Both VP+ and Director/Manager
Function diversity Multiple departments involved 3+ distinct functions
Tech + Business Builders and buyers both involved Technical AND business contacts

The Results: Completeness Predicts Wins

Completeness Tier Win Rate Accounts
Complete (75-100) 49% 9,356
High (50-75) 39% 10,467
Medium (25-50) 28% 11,029
Low (0-25) 22% 5,121

Accounts with complete buying groups win at 2.2x the rate of those with low completeness — and this pattern holds across thousands of accounts per tier. This isn’t surprising — it’s hard to close a deal when the right people aren’t at the table — but the magnitude and consistency of the difference gives marketing a clear mandate: invest in filling coverage gaps.

Gap Analysis: What’s Missing and What to Do About It

The analysis identified 29,512 accounts with specific coverage gaps. The most common gaps:

  • Missing buying roles (Champions and Decision Makers are the most commonly absent)
  • No VP+ contact engaged on the deal
  • Only one department represented (usually the team that initiated the conversation)

For each gap account, the system generates a specific enrichment recommendation: “This account needs a Champion in Engineering” or “No VP+ contact — target Director or VP-level outreach.”

Estimating Pipeline Value

The final step is quantifying the opportunity. The framework estimates pipeline value under explicit assumptions: a deal size of ~2% of account annual revenue, and a 10 percentage-point win rate uplift from filling coverage gaps.

The absolute dollar estimates from synthetic data are illustrative — in production, both assumptions would need calibration against real historical conversion data, and actual deal-to-revenue ratios and enrichment uplift vary significantly by segment, industry, and sales motion. The value of this step isn’t the specific number — it’s the framework for sizing the opportunity, which translates the analysis into a business case that executives can evaluate and pressure-test with their own assumptions.

The Targeting Framework

The most powerful view combines both layers — propensity scores and buying group completeness — into a 2×2 framework:

Complete Buying Group Gaps Exist
High Propensity Close now — sales should be all over these Enrich & close — highest-ROI enrichment targets
Low Propensity Nurture — engaged but signals are weak Low priority — gaps AND weak signals

The upper-right quadrant is the sweet spot: 7,068 accounts the model thinks are likely to buy, but where the buying group isn’t complete (30% win rate vs. 48% for high-propensity accounts with complete groups). These are the accounts where a targeted contact addition — finding the missing Champion, engaging the VP — could tip the deal.

What I’d Do Differently in Production

This project uses synthetic data and runs offline. In a production environment, several things would change:

  • Title normalization — Real job titles are messy (“Sr. Dir. of Eng” vs “Senior Director, Engineering”). I’d use fuzzy matching or an LLM to standardize before feature extraction.
  • Temporal validation — Instead of random train/test splits, I’d train on earlier time periods and test on later ones. This better simulates how the model would perform on genuinely unseen future data.
  • Live scoring pipeline — Integrate with Salesforce or HubSpot via scheduled batch scoring, so scores update automatically as new contacts and deal activity come in.
  • Model monitoring — Track AUC and calibration over time, with alerts when performance drops. Retrain quarterly or when the product/market changes significantly.
  • A/B test the model — Before full rollout, randomly assign accounts to model-scored vs. control groups and measure the incremental lift in pipeline and bookings. This is the gold standard for proving the model actually drives results, not just correlates with them.
