Interview Prep: Lead Scoring Project

Note

Private prep doc — not linked from site navigation.

The 60-Second Pitch

“I built an end-to-end lead scoring system for B2B SaaS marketing. It takes CRM data — accounts, contacts, deal history — and does two things: first, it scores every account on how likely they are to close, using a logistic regression model. Second, it analyzes the buying group at each account — who’s involved in the deal, what roles they play — and identifies specific coverage gaps. The punchline is that accounts with complete buying groups close at 2.2x the rate of those with gaps, and the gap analysis identifies specific enrichment targets with a framework for estimating pipeline value. It mirrors similar work I did at Atlassian, where I built the company’s first ICP and buying group analysis for the enterprise marketing org.”

Practice this until it’s natural. Hit the business framing first, then the method, then the result.

Likely Questions & How to Answer Them

“Walk me through how you built this.”

Your answer (2-3 minutes):

“I started with the data. In a real CRM you have four key tables — accounts with firmographic data like company size and industry, contacts with their job titles and seniority levels, opportunities which are the actual deals in progress, and then a bridge table that tells you which contacts are involved in which deals and what role they play — Champion, Decision Maker, Evaluator, and so on.

The feature engineering is where most of the value comes from. I built features at two levels for the propensity model, and a third level for the buying group analysis. First, account-level stuff — company size, industry, tech stack. Second, contact composition — rolling up who we know at each account. How many VPs do we have? Are we talking to multiple departments or just one? These are pre-deal features — they come from the contacts table, not from deal outcomes, so there’s no target leakage.

I deliberately kept the buying group features — how many people are on the deal, what roles are filled — separate from the propensity model. Those features come from the same deals whose outcome is the target variable, so including them would be target leakage. They’re used in the buying group analysis instead, where they’re descriptive rather than predictive.

For the model, I used logistic regression. I also trained a Random Forest to benchmark, and the performance difference was marginal, so I went with logistic regression because the coefficients are directly interpretable — you can tell marketing ‘VP engagement is your strongest signal’ and they can act on that.

For evaluation, I focused on business metrics — lift charts, precision at the top 10%, revenue capture curves — because AUC alone doesn’t answer the question marketing actually cares about, which is ‘if I can only target 100 accounts, which ones?’ I also added bootstrap confidence intervals for AUC and precision@10% so the numbers come with uncertainty estimates, not just point values.”

If they want more detail, they’ll ask. Don’t over-explain unprompted.

“Why logistic regression?”

Your answer:

“Three reasons. First, interpretability — the coefficients translate directly to a scoring system that marketing teams can understand and act on. ‘VP engagement adds X points’ is something a campaign manager can work with. Second, it’s what production marketing stacks actually use under the hood — Marketo, HubSpot, they’re all running logistic regression for lead scoring. So the approach is directly transferable. Third, I benchmarked against a Random Forest and the Random Forest actually performed slightly worse — AUC 0.567 vs 0.575 for logistic regression. At 100K-account scale, there’s no performance justification for losing interpretability.”

Follow-up you might get: “When would you use a more complex model?”

“If the AUC gap were larger — say 5+ points — I’d consider it, especially if the use case was purely automated scoring with no human in the loop. If someone needs to understand why an account is scored high, logistic regression wins. If it’s just feeding a downstream system and nobody looks at the coefficients, you can go complex.”

“What is AUC? What does 0.575 mean?”

What AUC is (in your words):

AUC stands for Area Under the ROC Curve. Here’s the intuition: if you pick one account that actually won and one that actually lost, AUC is the probability that the model scores the winner higher than the loser.

  • 0.5 = coin flip, model is useless
  • 1.0 = perfect separation, model never gets it wrong
  • 0.575 = the model ranks the winner higher than the loser about 58% of the time

Why 0.575 is actually okay:

“For account-level propensity models using only pre-deal CRM features, AUC in the 0.55-0.65 range is typical. A lot of what determines whether a deal closes — budget timing, internal politics, competitive dynamics — isn’t in the data. The model captures the structural signals that are in the data, and the business metrics show it concentrates value at the top: the top 10% of scored accounts have a 45% win rate vs 36% baseline. That’s actionable across 100K accounts. The 95% CI is tight (0.56–0.59) thanks to the large test set, so we know the performance is reliably above random.”

Honest framing if pushed: “AUC isn’t high enough that you’d blindly trust the model. It’s a tool that improves prioritization, not a crystal ball. That’s why I pair it with the buying group analysis — the model tells you who to focus on, the buying group analysis tells you what to do about it.”

“Explain precision at K.”

Your answer:

“Precision at K answers: if I take the top K% of model-scored accounts, what fraction actually won? Our top 10% has 45% precision — compared to a 36% baseline. At the top 20% and 30%, precision holds at 44% and 42% respectively. The practical takeaway: the model meaningfully concentrates wins in the top tiers. That’s fine for marketing because their question is usually ‘which 3,000 accounts should I target out of 100,000?’ — and the model gives them a 25% better hit rate than random targeting.”

“What are precision, recall, and K?”

These come up constantly. Here’s how to explain them without sounding like a textbook.

The setup — the confusion matrix:

Every prediction the model makes falls into one of four buckets:

Actually Won Actually Lost
Model said “win” True Positive (TP) — correctly identified a winner False Positive (FP) — predicted win, actually lost
Model said “lose” False Negative (FN) — missed a winner True Negative (TN) — correctly identified a loser

Precision answers: “Of the accounts the model flagged as winners, how many actually won?”

Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

In plain language: “When the model says an account will close, how often is it right?” High precision means few false alarms. If precision is 45%, that means 45% of the accounts the model calls winners actually are — the other 55% are false positives.

Why it matters for this project: Marketing has limited bandwidth. If they target the model’s top picks and half of them are duds, that’s wasted outreach. Precision tells you how efficient the model’s targeting is.

Recall answers: “Of all the accounts that actually won, how many did the model catch?”

Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

In plain language: “Of every deal that actually closed, what fraction did the model surface?” High recall means you’re not missing winners. If recall is 80%, the model caught 80% of the deals that closed — but 20% slipped through as false negatives.

Why it matters for this project: If marketing only targets model-recommended accounts and the model has low recall, they’re leaving pipeline on the table — deals they would have won but never engaged because the model didn’t flag them.

The precision-recall tradeoff:

You can’t maximize both simultaneously. If you lower the score threshold (call more accounts “winners”), you’ll catch more actual winners (recall goes up) but also flag more false positives (precision goes down). If you raise the threshold (only call very high-scoring accounts “winners”), precision goes up but you miss more real winners (recall drops).

“In lead scoring, we tend to care more about precision at the top — marketing wants to know ‘are the accounts I’m targeting actually good?’ rather than ‘am I catching every possible deal?’ That’s why we report precision@K rather than raw recall.”

K — what it means:

K is just “how deep into the ranked list are you going?” When we say “precision at K=10%,” we mean: take the top 10% of model-scored accounts, and measure what fraction actually won.

  • Precision@10% = take the top ~3,600 accounts (10% of 36K), 45% of them won
  • Precision@20% = take the top ~7,200 accounts, 44% won
  • Precision@30% = take the top ~10,800 accounts, 42% won
  • Baseline = if you picked randomly, 36% would have won

K matters because marketing doesn’t score every account equally — they want the best accounts. Precision@K tells you how good the model is at concentrating winners at the top of the list, which is the actual business question.

If they ask about F1 score:

“F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall — it balances both into a single number. It’s useful when you care equally about catching all positives and avoiding false alarms. For lead scoring, we don’t care equally — precision at the top matters more than overall recall, because marketing is selecting a subset to target, not trying to catch every possible deal. That’s why we use precision@K and lift charts instead of F1.”

“What is regularization? Why did you use it?”

What it is:

Regularization is a technique that prevents the model from overfitting — putting too much weight on any single feature or memorizing quirks of the training data. It adds a penalty to the model for large coefficients, which forces it to keep things simple.

L2 vs L1 (in case they ask):

  • L2 (Ridge) — shrinks all coefficients toward zero, but keeps them all in the model. Good when you think most features have some signal.
  • L1 (Lasso) — can shrink coefficients all the way to zero, effectively removing features. Good for feature selection when you suspect many features are noise.

“I used L2 because most of the features are grounded in real business logic — there’s a reason each one is there. I didn’t want the model to arbitrarily drop features; I wanted it to weight them appropriately.”

The C parameter:

“C controls how strong the regularization is. Lower C = stronger regularization = simpler model. I tested 0.01, 0.1, 1.0, and 10.0 through cross-validation, and 0.01 performed best — which makes sense with ~36K accounts and 39 features. The model benefits from being constrained — it keeps correlated features like employee count and revenue from inflating coefficients.”

“What is cross-validation?”

Your answer:

“Instead of evaluating the model on one fixed split of the data, cross-validation splits the training data into K chunks — I used 5. It trains on 4 chunks, evaluates on the 5th, and rotates through all 5 combinations. The average performance across all 5 gives you a more reliable estimate than a single split, because you’re not at the mercy of which particular accounts happened to land in the test set.”

“What is feature engineering?”

Your answer:

“It’s the process of turning raw data into signals the model can learn from. A company name or a job title string isn’t useful to a model. But ‘this account has 3 VP-level contacts across 4 different departments’ — that’s a signal. Feature engineering is where domain knowledge meets data science. Knowing that buying group composition matters for B2B deals is a business insight; encoding it as contact count, function diversity, and senior density is the engineering part.”

“What’s the buying group analysis and why does it matter?”

Your answer:

“In B2B, deals aren’t closed by one person. A typical enterprise deal has 4-7 people involved — someone championing the deal internally, someone who can sign the check, someone evaluating the technical fit, and people influencing the decision. If any of those roles are missing, the deal stalls.

I score every account’s buying group on a 0-100 scale across four dimensions: are the key roles filled, is there executive engagement, are multiple departments involved, and do we have both technical and business contacts. Accounts that score 75+ close at 49%. Accounts below 25 close at 22%. That’s a 2.2x difference — and it holds across thousands of accounts per tier.

The gap analysis then identifies what’s missing at each account — ‘you need a Champion at this account’ or ‘you have no VP-level engagement here.’ That turns a model score into a specific action for the marketing team.

The last step is sizing the opportunity. Under illustrative assumptions — a deal size of about 2% of account revenue, a 10 percentage-point win rate uplift from filling gaps — the framework estimates pipeline value from enrichment. The absolute dollar numbers depend on the synthetic revenue distributions, so I don’t cite a specific figure — but the framework for sizing the opportunity is the actual value. It translates the analysis into a business case executives can evaluate and pressure-test with their own assumptions.”

“How does this relate to what you did at Atlassian?”

Your answer:

“At Atlassian I pioneered the ICP and Buying Group analysis for the enterprise marketing org — it was a first for the team. I identified contact segments that were 5x more likely to progress through the funnel, found accounts with coverage gaps, and built enrichment strategies that informed marketing’s contact acquisition priorities.

This portfolio project recreates that methodology on synthetic data so I can walk through the full approach openly. The real work at Atlassian used internal data I can’t share, but the approach — scoring accounts on completeness, identifying gaps, building a framework for estimating the pipeline value of enrichment — is the same.”

“You used synthetic data. How do you know this would work on real data?”

Your answer:

“I want to be upfront about what the synthetic data does and doesn’t prove. The data generator embeds known signals at controlled strengths — VP presence, tech stack fit, buying group size all boost win probability. The model’s job is to recover those signals from the feature matrix. So this validates that the pipeline works end-to-end — the feature engineering, model training, and evaluation all do what they should. It demonstrates methodology, not empirical discovery.

The AUC of 0.575 reflects realistic signal strength — it’s not 0.95, which would be a red flag. Real data would add challenges: messy job titles that need normalization, missing fields, data entry inconsistencies, confounders, and temporal dynamics. The project addresses all of those in the production considerations. But the engineering and evaluation framework — the feature hierarchy, the target leakage separation, the business metrics — that carries over directly.”

“Tell me about the Streamlit dashboard.”

Your answer:

“It’s an interactive version of the notebook outputs. Four tabs — propensity scores with filtering by segment and industry, buying group analysis with the completeness-vs-win-rate charts, a gap analysis tab that surfaces the highest-ROI enrichment targets, and model details showing feature importance and lift charts. It’s self-contained — generates the data and trains the model in-memory, no API or database needed. Runs locally or on Streamlit Cloud.”

Concepts You Should Be Comfortable With

The ones you’ll definitely get asked about:

Concept One-sentence explanation
Logistic regression A model that predicts the probability of a yes/no outcome by learning a weighted sum of input features
AUC Probability that the model ranks a random positive higher than a random negative; 0.5 = random, 1.0 = perfect
Precision Of the items the model flagged as positive, what fraction actually are — measures false alarm rate
Recall Of all actual positives, what fraction did the model catch — measures how many winners you miss
Precision @ K Precision measured on only the top K% of scored items — the metric marketing actually cares about
Lift How much better the model is than random at a given threshold — 1.3x means 30% better
Regularization A penalty that keeps model coefficients small, preventing overfitting
Cross-validation Evaluate model performance by rotating through multiple train/test splits
Feature engineering Transforming raw data into numeric signals that capture domain knowledge
Overfitting When a model memorizes training data quirks instead of learning real patterns; performs well on training data but poorly on new data
Train/test split Holding out 20% of data the model never sees during training, to get an honest performance estimate
Stratified split Ensuring the train and test sets have the same ratio of wins to losses

The ones you might get asked about:

Concept One-sentence explanation
StandardScaler Centers features to mean=0 and std=1 so features on different scales (employee count vs 0/1 flags) are comparable
One-hot encoding Converting a category like “SMB/Mid-Market/Enterprise” into three separate 0/1 columns
Log transform Compresses right-skewed data (most values small, a few huge) into a more symmetric distribution
GridSearchCV Systematically tries different hyperparameter values and picks the one with best cross-validated performance
Pipeline Chains preprocessing (scaling) and the model together so they’re applied consistently to train and test data
Calibration Whether predicted probabilities are trustworthy — a model that says “40% chance” should be right ~40% of the time
ROC curve Plots true positive rate vs false positive rate at every score threshold; AUC is the area under this curve
Coefficients In logistic regression, the weight assigned to each feature. Positive = increases probability, negative = decreases it
Point-biserial correlation Correlation between a continuous variable and a binary variable — used to check feature-target relationships

Gotchas & Honest Answers

“Did you write all the code yourself?”

Honest answer: “I built this with AI assistance — I used Claude Code as a coding partner. I directed the architecture, made the analytical decisions (what features to build, which model to use, how to evaluate), and wrote the business framing. The AI helped with implementation — writing the sklearn pipelines, generating the synthetic data, structuring the tests. This is how I work as a data scientist — I use AI tools to extend my capabilities, which is why I can ship end-to-end projects like this faster than you’d expect.”

Why this is fine: This is literally the skill companies are hiring for. You’re demonstrating AI-augmented productivity, not pretending to be an ML engineer.

“Could you write logistic regression from scratch?”

Honest answer: “I understand the mechanics — it’s maximum likelihood estimation of a sigmoid function over a linear combination of features. I could walk through the math on a whiteboard. But in practice, nobody writes logistic regression from scratch — you use scikit-learn, and the value is in knowing when to use it, how to evaluate it, and what the output means for the business. That’s where I focus.”

“Why is the AUC only 0.575?”

Honest answer: “Account-level propensity models using only pre-deal CRM features typically land in the 0.55-0.65 range. The features that drive deal outcomes in B2B — budget timing, competitive landscape, internal champion strength — are only partially observable in CRM data. The model captures the structural signals that are available, and the business metrics show it concentrates value: top 10% accounts convert at 45% vs 36% baseline. Across 100K accounts, that’s a meaningful improvement for a marketing team deciding where to allocate resources. And the tight confidence interval (0.56–0.59) means we’re confident it’s real, not a lucky split.”

“This is synthetic data. Isn’t that cheating?”

Honest answer: “The synthetic data lets me show the full methodology without exposing proprietary Atlassian data. I’m upfront in the project that the data generator embeds signals the model then recovers — so this validates the pipeline, not discovery. The signals are calibrated to realistic strengths — the AUC is 0.575, not 0.95. Real data would add noise, confounders, and temporal dynamics. The project explicitly addresses what would change: title normalization, temporal validation, class imbalance, model monitoring, and A/B testing the model against a control group.”
