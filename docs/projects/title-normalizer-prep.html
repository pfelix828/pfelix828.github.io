Interview Prep: Title Normalizer Project

Note

Private prep doc — not linked from site navigation.

The 60-Second Pitch

“I built a model that cleans up messy CRM job titles. In any B2B marketing database, you’ll have thousands of variations — ‘Sr. Dir. Mktg’, ‘SENIOR DIRECTOR ENGINEERING’, ‘Snr. Direcotr, Engg (EMEA)’ — and they all need to be standardized before you can do anything useful with them, like segmentation or buying group analysis. My model takes any messy title and classifies it into a seniority level and a job function — so ‘Sr. Dir. Mktg’ becomes ‘senior director, marketing.’ It gets both right about 89% of the time on intentionally noisy data that mirrors real CRM exports. This is the same kind of preprocessing I had to do at Atlassian before I could build the contact segmentation work.”

Practice this until it’s natural. Lead with the business problem, then what the model does, then the result. Keep it simple — if they want to go deeper on the technical side, they’ll ask.

Likely Questions & How to Answer Them

“Walk me through how you built this.”

Your answer (2-3 minutes):

“The problem is that CRM data is messy. You get ‘Sr. Dir. of Eng’, ‘SENIOR DIRECTOR ENGINEERING’, ‘Snr. Direcotr, Engg (EMEA)’ — these are all the same role but they look completely different to a computer. I needed to map every title to two things: a seniority level — one of ten, from individual contributor up to C-suite — and a job function — one of ten, like engineering, marketing, sales.

First, I built a data generator that creates 20,000 fake but realistic job titles with the same kinds of mess you see in real CRM exports — misspellings, abbreviations, titles that got cut off because the CRM field wasn’t long enough, geographic tags like ‘(EMEA)’ tacked on, people putting their MBA after their title. I wanted the training data to be ugly, because real data is ugly.

Then I trained a model that takes in a messy title and outputs two predictions at once — the seniority and the function. The reason it predicts both at the same time instead of using two separate models is that the two things are related. ‘CTO’ tells you both seniority and function in one word. By having one model learn both tasks, it picks up on those connections.

I also built a simple baseline first — basically just counting which words appear in which titles and using that to predict. The more sophisticated model beats the baseline, especially on the hardest cases where titles are really mangled.”

If they want more detail, they’ll ask. Don’t over-explain unprompted.

“Why did you use a neural network for this?”

Your answer:

“The main advantage is that the model learns that different words mean the same thing. If you write rules by hand — ‘Sr.’ means ‘Senior’, ‘Dir.’ means ‘Director’ — you’re always chasing edge cases. The model learns these relationships automatically from the data. After training, it figures out on its own that ‘VP’, ‘V.P.’, and ‘Vice President’ all mean the same thing, without me having to spell out every abbreviation.

The simpler approach — just counting word frequencies — treats ‘VP’ and ‘Vice President’ as completely unrelated. The neural network can learn they’re the same concept.”

Follow-up you might get: “When would you not use a neural network for this?”

“If the titles were already pretty clean, or if I only had a few hundred examples. The neural network needs enough data to learn those word relationships. With very little data, the simpler word-counting approach would actually work better.”

“How does the model predict both seniority and function at the same time?”

Your answer:

“Think of it like this: the model first reads the title and builds an internal summary of what it means — like a compressed understanding. Then that summary gets passed to two separate decision-makers. One looks at the summary and decides the seniority. The other looks at the same summary and decides the function.

The key insight is that both decision-makers benefit from the same summary. If the model learns that ‘CTO’ means something important, that knowledge helps both the seniority prediction (C-suite) and the function prediction (engineering/technology). It’s more efficient than building two completely separate models, and in practice it’s also slightly more accurate because the tasks help each other learn.”

“You mentioned two model types — what’s the difference?”

Your answer:

“I tried two different approaches for how the model reads a title:

The first one reads the title word by word, left to right and also right to left, keeping track of what it’s seen — like reading a sentence. This is good because ‘VP of Engineering’ and ‘Engineering VP’ both get understood correctly regardless of word order.

The second one works more like pattern matching. It scans the title looking for specific word combinations — two-word patterns like ‘VP of’, three-word patterns like ‘Director of Engineering’. It’s asking ‘do I see any patterns I recognize?’ This one turned out to be slightly better for job titles because the useful information is usually in short word combinations, not in the overall sentence structure.

Both beat the simple baseline, and both are reasonable choices. I went with the pattern-matching one because it was a bit more accurate and faster to train.”

If they push for the technical names:

“The first is called a BiLSTM — bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory. The second is a CNN — Convolutional Neural Network. CNNs are more commonly known for image recognition, but the same idea of ‘scan for local patterns’ works well on short text like job titles.”

“How does the model learn that ‘Sr.’ and ‘Senior’ mean the same thing?”

Your answer:

“When the model starts training, every word is just a random blob of numbers — it has no idea what anything means. But as it trains on thousands of examples, it notices that ‘Sr.’ and ‘Senior’ appear in the same kinds of titles, surrounded by the same kinds of other words, and leading to the same predictions. So it gradually moves their internal representations closer together.

After training, ‘Senior’, ‘Sr.’, ‘Snr’, and ‘Snr.’ all end up in similar positions in the model’s internal space. The model figured this out on its own — I didn’t write any rules telling it those are the same. This is the main advantage over a rule-based approach, where you’d have to manually list every possible abbreviation.”

“How did you handle the messy data? What kinds of noise?”

Your answer:

“I studied what real CRM exports look like and built a generator that creates the same kinds of problems. About 15 categories of noise:

The easy stuff — abbreviations like ‘Sr.’ for ‘Senior’, random capitalization, extra spaces, and typos where letters get swapped.

But also the stuff that actually makes CRM data painful: titles that got cut off mid-word because the CRM field only allows 30 characters — so ‘Senior Vice President of Engineering’ becomes ‘Senior Vice Presi…’ and now you’re missing the key information. Geographic tags that got appended — ‘(EMEA)’ or ‘- North America.’ People putting their credentials after their title — ‘, MBA’ or ‘, PMP.’ Real misspellings that aren’t just letter swaps — ‘Manger’ instead of ‘Manager’, ‘Engineeer’ with a triple e. Words in the wrong order. Copy-paste junk from system imports — tab characters and em dashes that come from pasting out of Word docs or web forms. The tokenizer specifically handles those edge cases.

Each fake title gets a random mix of these problems, so the model has to handle combinations it hasn’t seen before.”

Why this matters for the interview:

“This is where domain knowledge shows. Anyone can train a text classifier. Knowing that CRM fields get truncated at 30 characters, or that someone’s LinkedIn import includes their MBA in the title field — that comes from actually working with this data.”

“Why is Product Manager labeled as Individual Contributor?”

Your answer:

“Great catch — this is actually a deliberate taxonomy decision that shows why title normalization is hard. In most tech companies, ‘Product Manager’ is a function descriptor, not a seniority marker. A PM is an IC role — the people-management equivalent is ‘Director of Product’ or ‘VP of Product.’ Same with ‘Social Media Manager’ in marketing — the word ‘Manager’ describes what you manage, not that you manage people.

This is the exact kind of ambiguity that makes title normalization genuinely difficult. ‘Manager’ means different things in different contexts. In production, you’d want to surface these edge cases through confidence monitoring and get human input to calibrate the taxonomy for each company’s conventions.”

“What happens if someone has a title the model has never seen?”

Your answer:

“It would misclassify or give a low-confidence prediction. Some seniority/function combinations don’t even exist in the training data — there’s no ‘SVP of Design’ template, because that title is rare in practice. So the model has literally never seen that pattern.

In production, this is why confidence monitoring matters. The model outputs probability distributions, not just labels. If it says ‘45% VP, 40% Director,’ that’s a signal to route to human review. I’d implement active learning — flag low-confidence predictions, have humans correct them, and use those corrections to retrain. Over time, the model covers more and more of the edge cases it initially missed.”

“What’s the baseline and why did you build it?”

Your answer:

“Before building anything complex, I built the simplest thing that could possibly work. I used a standard technique that converts each title into a list of numbers based on which words appear in it, weighting rare words higher than common ones. Then I used a basic statistical model to predict from those numbers. This is called TF-IDF with Logistic Regression — it’s a well-known, off-the-shelf approach.

The reason you always do this first is so you have something to compare against. The neural network beats it — 89% vs 87.6% on getting both predictions right — but I want to be honest: that’s a 1.4 percentage point gap. TF-IDF trains in seconds, needs no GPU, and is trivially deployable. The neural network requires PyTorch, embedding layers, and a training loop.

So when is the complexity worth it? At scale. On a database of 100K contacts, 1.4 percentage points is ~1,400 additional correct classifications. And the gap is concentrated in the hardest cases — abbreviated titles, misspellings, reordered titles — where learned embeddings capture similarity that bag-of-words misses. In production, I’d start with TF-IDF and upgrade to the CNN only if those marginal error cases matter for downstream analysis quality.”

This is a strong talking point. It shows you think about cost-benefit tradeoffs, not just accuracy numbers. If they push on it, say: “I’d rather ship a simple model that works than spend weeks optimizing a neural network for 1.4 points.”

“How does this connect to your Atlassian work?”

Your answer:

“At Atlassian, I built the contact segmentation and buying group analysis for enterprise marketing. Before I could do any of that, I needed to know things like: is this person a VP or an IC? Are they in engineering or marketing? That meant normalizing messy job titles — the exact problem this project solves.

This project isolates that preprocessing step as a standalone system. The seniority and function labels it produces are exactly the features that feed into the lead scoring and buying group models in my other portfolio project. Together they form the full pipeline: clean up titles → build contact features → score accounts → find gaps in buying groups.”

“You used synthetic data. How realistic is this?”

Your answer:

“The accuracy numbers — 89-95% depending on which prediction — are in a realistic range. I deliberately made the noise aggressive enough that the model doesn’t hit 99%, because that would mean the data is too clean compared to reality.

The biggest limitation is that all noise is applied to a fixed set of about 500 canonical title templates. The model never encounters a title structure it hasn’t seen some variant of. Real CRM data includes titles outside any taxonomy — ‘Chief Evangelist,’ ‘Growth Hacker,’ ‘Sustainability Officer’ — plus non-English titles, hybrid roles like ‘Marketing Engineer,’ and genuinely ambiguous titles like ‘Director’ with no function context. The distribution would also be heavily skewed — way more ICs than C-suite.

I’d expect accuracy to drop 5-10 points on real CRM data. That’s still valuable — even 80% accuracy on automated title normalization saves hundreds of hours of manual work per quarter. And you’d route the low-confidence predictions to human review through active learning.”

“How do you know the model isn’t just memorizing the training data?”

Your answer:

“Three safeguards. First, I use a proper three-way split: training data, a validation set, and a held-out test set. The model trains on the training set, the validation set is used to decide when to stop training, and the test set is only used once at the end for the final accuracy numbers. This prevents any information from the evaluation leaking into the training process.

Second, I use early stopping. During training, I check performance on the validation set after every pass. If the model stops improving for 5 rounds in a row, I stop training and roll back to the best version. This prevents the model from training too long and starting to memorize quirks of the training data instead of learning general patterns.

Third, the accuracy numbers I report are all on the held-out test set — data the model never saw during training or during the early stopping decisions. That gives you the most honest estimate of real-world performance.”

Concepts You Should Be Comfortable With

The ones you’ll definitely get asked about:

Concept What to say
Neural network “A model that learns patterns from data by adjusting thousands of internal settings until its predictions match the right answers. Think of it as learning by trial and error — it starts wrong, checks its answers, and gradually improves.”
Training “Showing the model thousands of examples with known answers so it can learn the patterns. Like flash cards — you show it a messy title and the correct seniority, over and over, until it gets good at it.”
Overfitting “When the model memorizes the training examples instead of learning general patterns. It gets great scores on data it’s seen, but falls apart on new data. Like studying only the practice test and bombing the real exam.”
Train/validation/test split “A three-way split. The model trains on the training set, the validation set decides when to stop training (early stopping), and the test set is only touched once at the end for final evaluation. This prevents any evaluation leakage into training.”
Baseline “The simplest approach that could possibly work. You build this first so you can prove the fancier model is worth the added complexity.”
Accuracy “What percentage of predictions the model gets right. Our model gets seniority right 95% of the time, function right 93% of the time, and both right 89% of the time.”

The ones you might get asked about:

Concept What to say
Embeddings “A way for the model to learn that different words mean the same thing. After training, ‘VP’, ‘V.P.’, and ‘Vice President’ all get treated as basically the same word internally.”
Tokenization “Chopping a title into individual words so the model can process them. ‘Senior VP of Sales’ becomes [‘senior’, ‘vp’, ‘of’, ‘sales’].”
Multi-task “One model predicting two things at once (seniority and function) instead of using two separate models. Works better because the tasks help each other learn.”
Early stopping “Automatically stopping training when the model stops improving, to prevent overfitting. Like knowing when to stop studying — more isn’t always better.”
Epoch “One complete pass through all the training data. The model typically needs 15-25 passes to learn well.”
TF-IDF “A way to convert text into numbers by counting words, weighting rare words higher. ‘Compliance’ is more distinctive than ‘Manager’, so it gets a higher weight.”
Logistic regression “A simple statistical model that predicts a category from numeric inputs. The standard first thing to try for classification problems.”
Precision and recall “Precision: of the ones the model predicted as ‘VP’, how many actually were? Recall: of all the actual VPs, how many did the model find?”
F1 score “A single number that balances precision and recall. High F1 means the model is both accurate and thorough.”

Gotchas & Honest Answers

“Did you write all the code yourself?”

Honest answer: “I built this with AI assistance — I used Claude Code as a coding partner. I directed the decisions: what problem to solve, how to structure the data, what model approaches to try, what makes realistic CRM noise. The AI helped implement those decisions in code. This is how I work — I use AI tools to move faster, and the value I bring is knowing what to build and why.”

Why this is fine: You’re demonstrating judgment and domain knowledge, not typing speed. The hard part isn’t writing code — it’s knowing that CRM titles need 15 types of noise to be realistic, or that predicting seniority and function together works better than separately.

“Could you explain how the neural network works under the hood?”

Honest answer: “At a high level — the model converts each word into numbers, processes them through layers that detect patterns, and outputs a prediction. I understand the workflow and can explain the concepts. I’m not an ML engineer who implements neural networks from scratch — I’m a data scientist who knows when to use them, how to evaluate them, and what the results mean for the business. In practice, you use frameworks like PyTorch that handle the low-level math.”

“Why are the accuracy numbers in the 89-95% range and not higher?”

Honest answer: “By design. The training data includes aggressive noise — truncated titles, real misspellings, geographic tags, credential suffixes — because that’s what real CRM data looks like. If the model hit 99%, it would mean my test data was unrealistically clean. 89% combined accuracy means the model gets both predictions right on 9 out of 10 titles. The other 10% would get flagged for human review in production.”

“Is the neural network really worth it over the baseline?”

Honest answer: “The gap is 1.4 percentage points — 89% vs 87.6%. If you asked me ‘should we ship the CNN or the TF-IDF?’ my honest answer is: start with TF-IDF. It trains in seconds, needs no GPU, and gets you 87.6%. Upgrade to the CNN if you’re processing enough contacts that 1.4% matters — at 100K contacts, that’s 1,400 more correct classifications, concentrated in the hardest cases. I built both because the comparison is the point: know what simple gives you before investing in complexity.”

“This is synthetic data. Isn’t that cheating?”

Honest answer: “It’s a limitation I’m upfront about in the project. I can’t share real Atlassian CRM data, so I built a generator that mirrors the same kinds of data quality problems. The noise is realistic — that’s why accuracy is 89% and not 99%. The main limitation is the closed vocabulary: the model only sees variants of about 500 canonical titles. Real data would include titles outside any taxonomy, non-English titles, and hybrid roles the model has never encountered. I’d expect a 5-10 point accuracy drop until the model is retrained on real labeled data. But the pipeline — the architecture, the multi-task approach, the evaluation framework — transfers directly.”

Questions YOU Should Ask (That Show You Know This Space)

  • “How does your team currently handle title normalization — is it rule-based, a vendor solution, or manual?”
  • “What downstream systems use the normalized titles — does it feed into a scoring model, segmentation, or routing logic?”
  • “How often would something like this need retraining? If you expand into a new market with different title conventions, how would you know the model is struggling?”
  • “What’s the cost of a wrong prediction here — is misclassifying a VP as a Director worse than the reverse for your use case?”
