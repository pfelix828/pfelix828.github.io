AI Search Visibility Scorecard

Live App Source

What This Project Does

AI assistants now answer questions like “what’s the best PDF editor?” directly, and brands have no Search Console for that channel: no impression counts, no rank tracking, and answers that change every time you ask. This project is a working measurement framework for brand visibility in AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Google AI Overviews, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot), built as the weekly scorecard a growth marketing analytics team would actually run.

It tracks five Adobe products against their competitive categories (Adobe Express vs Canva, Firefly vs Midjourney, Acrobat vs Smallpdf, and so on) on fully simulated data. The dataset is fake by design and labeled as such on every page; the measurement methodology is real and cited to published industry practice.

The Measurement Problem

Traditional search measurement assumes a ranked list of links and a click you can attribute. Answer engines break all three assumptions:

  • No impressions or ranks. The engine synthesizes one answer; either your brand is in it or it isn’t, and nobody reports how often it was shown.
  • Stochastic answers. The same prompt returns a different brand list almost every run. Published research found the same brand list appears in fewer than 1 in 100 repeated runs, so a single check of “are we in the answer” is meaningless.
  • Broken attribution. Most AI-influenced visits lose their referrer on the way to your site. Clicks from Google AI Overviews arrive looking like ordinary Google traffic.

So the framework treats visibility like a survey instead of a log: sample repeatedly, estimate with uncertainty, and connect to business outcomes through stated assumptions rather than pretending click-path attribution exists.

How It Works

The prompt panel

The unit of measurement is a fixed panel of 40 prompts across 5 product categories, tagged by intent stage (discovery, evaluation, how-to, suitability) and weighted by estimated ask volume. Each prompt is re-asked many times per engine per week, mirroring how production tools like Adobe LLM Optimizer license tracked prompts.

Sampling with honest uncertainty

Mention rates are binomial estimates from 70 simulated runs per prompt, engine, and week, and every rate carries a Wilson 95% confidence interval. The interval is the point: 16 mentions in 20 runs reads as 80%, but the honest range is 58% to 92%. Weekly moves inside the interval are reported as noise, not news.

The metric set

Mention rate, share of voice against tracked competitors, average position in the answer, citation share (whether your domain is the cited source, which is a different event from being named), sentiment conditional on mention, and a composite Visibility Score with openly stated weights. The vocabulary deliberately matches Adobe LLM Optimizer’s published terms so the demo speaks the language of the production tool category.

From visibility to revenue

The Outcomes page builds a proxy chain from visibility to sessions to signups to an annualized new-revenue figure, with every assumption exposed as a slider and a sensitivity table showing which assumption moves the answer most. It also models the dark-traffic gap: the difference between what an analytics referrer report would credit and the modeled true AI-influenced traffic.

Experiments without a clean ground truth

You cannot randomize what ChatGPT says, so the experiment design uses treated-vs-control prompt panels with pooled pre/post reads. The demo includes one content experiment with a clear lift against a flat control, and one PR experiment with an honestly reported null on one engine. The null is presented as a feature of the process: it narrows the playbook.

Methodology page

The centerpiece for anyone evaluating the work: metric formulas, panel design, ten documented limitations (dark traffic, panel representativeness, model-version churn, vendor self-reporting), full data provenance, and references to the published research the framework is grounded in.

Data Honesty

All numbers in the app are generated by a seeded, deterministic script with binomial sampling at the prompt level, so confidence intervals and experiment reads behave the way real sampled data would. No real answer engines were scraped, no Adobe data was used, and nothing in the app is a claim about real brand performance, including the competitor gaps. This is an independent portfolio project, not affiliated with or endorsed by Adobe; product names are used only to make the measurement framework concrete.

What I’d Do Differently

  • Real sampling pipeline. Swap the simulated sampler for scheduled runs against engine APIs with the same statistics. The framework transfers; the plumbing changes.
  • Demand-validated panel. A fixed prompt list under-samples how differently real users phrase the same intent; the panel should be validated against real query and prompt-volume data.
  • Outcome-calibrated weights. The Visibility Score weights are stated demo choices. The production version should calibrate them against what actually predicts referred sessions and signups.
